LeakCold outreach — objection handling
The Service Edit

Every stall, and the exact words back.

Six objections a cafe owner will throw at you. A reframe for each, then a word-for-word script. Read the reframe to understand it; say the script.

◆
The rule: never argue. Agree first, then reframe. "Fair enough" lowers the guard; the reframe moves the goalposts from price to the cost of doing nothing. You are not selling an audit. You are selling the money they are already losing every month they wait.
1

Price — "$999 is too much"

"That's steep for me right now."

Reframe

It is not a cost. It is a diagnostic on money already leaving the till. The fee is fixed. The leak is not — it compounds every month it goes unfound.

Say this

"Fair. Quick question though: do you know exactly how much margin you lost last month to overpouring, mispriced items and roster overspend? You can't, because it's invisible from the floor. The audit's $999 once. The average leak I find is [$X,XXX] a month. So it's not whether you can afford the audit — it's whether you can afford to keep bleeding that every month you don't."

2

Time — "It'll take too long"

"I don't have time for a big project."

Reframe

It's done-for-you. They picture doing the work. They aren't — you are. Their total input is one short call and view-only access.

Say this

"You're not doing the audit — I am. I need one 30-minute call and read-only access to your numbers. That's your entire involvement. You get the full report back in [X business days]. It's the fastest hour of admin you'll do all year."

3

"I already have an accountant"

"My accountant handles all that."

Reframe

Different job. An accountant records history and files tax — they tell you what already happened. They don't sit in your operations hunting live leaks: portion drift, menu pricing, labour ratio, supplier creep. It isn't their role, and they aren't paid to.

Say this

"Perfect — keep them. Your accountant is the scoreboard: they tell you what the game was. I'm the coach: I find the margin leaking out right now and show you how to stop it. They keep you compliant. I make you money. You want both."

4

"We already use Xero"

"We've got all our numbers in Xero."

Reframe

Xero stores the data. It doesn't interpret it. It shows you a number, never why it's wrong or what to do about it. Xero is the filing cabinet; the audit is the person who reads the file.

Say this

"Great — that makes my job faster. Xero holds the raw data. It won't tell you your flat white is underpriced by 40 cents, or your Tuesday roster is overstaffed by two hours, or a supplier quietly lifted prices 9%. That's what the audit does with your Xero data. You've already done the hard part — I just read what it's telling you."

5

Too busy — "I'm flat out right now"

"Come back in a few months, we're slammed."

Reframe

Busy and profitable are not the same. Being too busy to check where you're bleeding is exactly why the leak keeps growing. "Too busy" is usually the symptom, not the reason to wait.

Say this

"I hear that from every owner — and it's the tell. You're working flat out and the margin still isn't landing, because it's leaking somewhere you can't see while you're on the floor. Waiting three months doesn't pause the leak, it funds it. The audit is the thing that buys your time back."

6

"I need to check with my partner"

"I'll need to run it past my business partner."

Reframe

Reasonable — it's a joint call. Don't fight it. Arm the person in front of you so they sell it for you, and put the numbers in their hand.

Say this

"Makes total sense — it should be a shared call. Here's the one line to take to them: $999 to find and fix the margin we're losing every month, with a report we keep and act on. Want me to send a one-page summary you can forward, so they see the numbers themselves? Or easiest — grab 15 minutes with the three of us and I'll answer them direct."

◆
If nothing lands: "No problem. Can I ask — is it the timing, the money, or you're just not convinced there's a leak to find? Whatever it is, tell me straight and I'll either fix it or leave you be." Naming the real objection out loud is where most deals actually turn.
