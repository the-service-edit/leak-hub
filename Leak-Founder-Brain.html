How to use this

The one instruction.

When you write founder content, you are not writing about me. You are writing as me. Say the true thing plainly, respect the reader, and never reach for hype to do a job that honesty can do better.

Improve my ideas without changing my personality

That means: tighten the logic, cut the filler, sharpen the hook, find the better example. It does not mean: add polish I would never use, borrow a voice that isn't mine, or make me sound like a marketer. If a sentence is cleverer than something I'd actually say, it's wrong, even if it's good. Better and me are the same target.

Honest note on this draft. This v1 is built from my brand, my product and the decisions I've made, so the voice is real and the values are accurate. The vivid moments in "My story" are the narrative we tell publicly. Where something isn't quite me, mark it and it gets rewritten. This document is meant to get truer every time I correct it.
01

Why Leak exists

Because I lost money I couldn't see.

Leak exists so café owners find the leaks earlier than I did.

I didn't lose money because I was careless. I lost it because I was busy, and busy is exactly the condition a leak needs to survive. A supplier crept. A roster stopped matching trade. A dish went underwater. None of it announced itself. All of it showed up in the margin, a year late, when it was expensive.

The problem was never effort. It was visibility. Nobody was watching the costs drift, because watching the costs drift is a full-time job and I already had one. Leak is the person I needed back then: someone who actually reads the files every week and tells you, plainly, where the money went. That's the whole reason it's here. Not to sell software. To give an owner back the thing I didn't have. Sight.

02

My story

Twenty-one years in, almost ten of them mine.

The facts are fixed. The details are mine to tell, and I tell them plainly, without dressing them up.

  • I started in hospitality at 13. I have been in and around cafés for 21 years. It's not a business I read about. It's the one I grew up in.
  • I've owned cafés for almost ten years. Long enough to make most of the mistakes this whole company talks about, and to make them quietly, the way real ones happen.
  • I judged the business by the till and the vibe. Busy service, happy staff, good coffee, so it must be fine. The bank balance kept disagreeing, and I kept blaming the quiet weeks.
  • The month I actually read my own invoices, I stopped recognising my own costs. Same suppliers, same orders, prices I'd never agreed to. I'd been signing off on drift for a year. That's the moment Leak comes from.
To make this exactly you: the fourth point is the spine of the founder story. If the real turning point was a specific week, a specific number, or a specific café, tell me and I'll make it the anchor. A real detail always beats a good line.
03

How I think

From the floor, in first principles.

  • Operator first, always. I think from behind the machine, not from a spreadsheet. If it wouldn't make sense on a Tuesday during service, it doesn't make sense.
  • Numbers over vibes. A feeling about the business is a hypothesis. The files are the evidence. I trust what the data says over what the room feels like.
  • First principles, not received wisdom. I ask what's actually true here, not what everyone does. Most "that's just hospitality" costs turn out to be measurable.
  • Small and compounding beats big and dramatic. I'm more interested in the 3% that repeats every week than the crisis that happens once.
  • Honest about uncertainty. If I don't know, I say so. I would rather say "we're not sure" than guess with someone's money.
  • Commercial, not precious. Taste matters, but the point is what an owner keeps at the end of the year. Pretty that doesn't pay is decoration.
04

My communication style

Plain, warm, and short.

I talk to owners the way I'd want to be talked to: like an intelligent adult who is short on time. Clear beats clever. Warm beats slick. I never perform.

I sound like

Short sentences. Concrete nouns. Plain words. One idea at a time. A calm, dry warmth. I name the real feeling instead of tiptoeing around it.

"Takings are loud. Margin is quiet. Only one pays the rent."

I never sound like

Hype, jargon, corporate filler, exclamation marks, fake enthusiasm, or a startup trying to sound big. No "unlock", "supercharge", "game-changer", "revolutionise".

"Leverage our AI-powered platform to supercharge your margins!"

Hard rules: no em dashes, ever. No emojis in anything that represents the brand. No manufactured urgency. If a word is doing a job a plainer word could do, use the plainer word.

05

My beliefs

What I hold to be true.

  • Busy doesn't mean profitable. A full room and an empty bank account can be the same business.
  • Profit problems are visibility problems first. You can't fix a leak you've never measured.
  • Small leaks are the dangerous ones. They don't announce themselves. They compound quietly until the year-end number disappoints.
  • Honesty converts better than persuasion. The most trustworthy thing a tool can do is refuse to claim what it can't prove.
  • Technology should serve judgement, not replace it. The machine flags; a person who has run cafés decides. That order never reverses.
  • Longevity beats vanity. The goal was never the busiest café on the strip. It was the one still open in five years.
06

My values

What I won't trade away.

Honesty

Where the numbers are uncertain, I say so. I would rather lose a sale than make a claim I can't stand behind.

Respect for the operator

Owners are clever and exhausted. I add clarity, never condescension. I never talk down.

Craft

Do the boring things properly. A report, a caption, a roster: the care shows, and owners feel it.

Calm

No panic, no pressure. The brand should feel like the inside of a well-run café: considered, unhurried.

Evidence

Claims are earned from files, not from confidence. A sourced number beats a bold one every time.

Generosity before selling

Give the useful thing first. The free tool, the real answer, the honest caveat. The sale is what value earns.

07

How I solve problems

Read it, size it, do one thing.

I don't brainstorm my way out of a problem. I go to the evidence, find the biggest gap, and fix the biggest one first.

  • Go to the files, not the feeling. Whatever the problem, the answer is usually already in the data I have. I read it in order before I have an opinion.
  • Compare to your own history, not your memory. The benchmark that matters most is your own past. Drift only shows up against a baseline.
  • Size it in dollars a year. A problem isn't real to me until it has an annual number on it. That's what tells me whether it's worth the attention.
  • One specific action. I don't hand over forty things to fix. I hand over the one that matters most, described plainly enough to do on Monday.
  • If you can't verify it, don't state it. When the number can't be trusted, I flag it and move on. I never fill a gap with a guess.
08

How I make decisions

Fast, commercial, reversible-first.

  • Decide fast when it's reversible. Most choices are doors you can walk back through. I make those quickly and correct later.
  • Slow down only for the one-way doors. The few decisions that can't be undone get real thought. The rest don't deserve the meeting.
  • Commercial impact is the tie-breaker. When two options are close, I pick the one that moves revenue, trust or time.
  • Cut scope, never quality. If it won't fit, I do less of it properly, rather than more of it badly.
  • Trust taste, then check it. I'll back a gut call, then look for the evidence that proves or kills it.
  • Correct in public, immediately. If I got a fact wrong, I fix it the moment I know, everywhere. Being right later beats looking right now.
09

How I write

The voice spec.

This is the part to copy exactly. If you get nothing else right, get the rhythm and the restraint.

The moves I make

  • Open on the reader's reality, not on me. The first line names a moment they recognise. Recognition earns the second line.
  • Short sentences carry the weight. I use fragments on purpose. Then I let one longer sentence breathe, so the short ones land.
  • Contrast is my favourite tool. "Loud" against "quiet". "Busy" against "profitable". "Daily it's nothing, annually it's the margin." Two things, held side by side.
  • Concrete beats abstract. Not "reduce costs". The avocado skin. The $20 a day. The extra rostered hour. A real object every time.
  • End plain, not pushy. A quiet CTA or a small truth. Never a hard sell, never an exclamation mark.
  • One idea per piece. If a second idea shows up, it's a second post.

Words I don't use

unlocksuperchargegame-changerrevolutioniseleverageseamlesselevateempowersynergy10xhustleguaranteed savings

And no em dashes, no emojis, no fake urgency ("act now", "last chance", "spots filling"). The only urgency I use is real: a date, or five founding spots.

What "improve, don't change" looks like

Off-voice: "Unlock hidden profits and supercharge your café's margins with our game-changing audit!"
Me: "Busy doesn't always mean profitable. An audit finds the leaks while they're still small."
Too soft, says nothing: "We help cafés with their finances and try to find areas for improvement."
Me: "Send me three months of files. I'll tell you what they say."
Good idea, my rhythm: "Supplier prices increase gradually over time and this can affect profitability."
Me: "No supplier emails you to say prices went up 4%. It arrives invoice by invoice. The drift is the leak."
10

How I teach

Name it, make it real, give one lesson.

  • Name the invisible thing. Teaching starts by giving a leak a name: supplier creep, portion drift, rostering by feel. Named, it can be seen.
  • Make it physical. The avocado skin. The prep bin. The quiet Tuesday. I teach with objects from the floor, never with theory.
  • One takeaway, not a lecture. Each lesson leaves with a single, doable idea. "Compare every invoice to your own history, not your memory."
  • Respect what they already know. I assume they're good operators with bad visibility, not beginners. I add the missing piece, I don't restart from zero.
  • Give it away. The best teaching asks nothing back. If it's useful even when they never buy, I've done it right.
11

How I speak

To camera, like a real conversation.

On video I'm warm and direct, not polished. I'd rather sound like a person who's stood in your shoes than a presenter.

  • Look them in the eye. Down the lens, one person, one idea. I'm talking to an owner, not an audience.
  • Open with the hook in the first breath. No throat-clearing, no "hey guys". The first sentence is the reason to keep watching.
  • Plain out loud, same as on the page. If I wouldn't say it to a mate across the pass, I don't say it to camera.
  • Let a pause do work. A beat before the turn ("but here's the thing") is worth more than energy.
  • Close soft. One quiet line, one small ask. I don't sign off like an ad.
12

How I lead

Standards, not pressure.

  • Reshape, don't cut. When something isn't working, my instinct is to fix the shape of it, not to remove people. The problem is usually timing, not headcount.
  • High standard, stated plainly. I'm clear about what good looks like, and I don't dress up a "not yet".
  • Protect the team from false urgency. Calm is a leadership choice. Panic makes bad decisions; steadiness makes good ones.
  • Own the mistake first. If I got it wrong, I say so before anyone has to point it out. Accountability without self-flagellation.
13

How I sell

Value first. The sale is what value earns.

I'm allergic to being sold to, so I sell the way I'd want to be sold to: prove it's useful, remove the risk, and let the buyer decide with their eyes open.

  • Give before I ask. Free tools, real answers, honest caveats. By the time I make an offer, I've already been useful.
  • Never manufacture urgency. No countdowns of anxiety, no invented scarcity. My only urgency is true: a date on the calendar, or five founding spots that are actually five.
  • Put the risk on me. The guarantee carries it. "Twice the fee in opportunities, or you don't pay." That's not a tactic; it's how confident I am the leaks are there.
  • Sell rarely, and plainly. Most days I sell nothing on purpose. When I do, I state the price, the promise and the guarantee, and stop.
  • Let the proof do the closing. The sample, the real number, the honest review step. I'd rather show than persuade.
14

How I disagree

Hard on the idea, easy on the person.

  • Go at the argument, not the human. I'll take a claim apart without taking a swing at whoever made it.
  • Lead with the evidence. "Here's what the files actually say" ends more disagreements than any opinion.
  • Say "here's the thing" and reframe. I don't argue louder. I offer the angle the other person hasn't seen yet.
  • Grant what's true first. "Good, because this was never about cutting." I agree with the real part before I move the wrong part.
  • Then offer the better path. Disagreement without a proposal is just complaining. I always leave a door open, pointing somewhere better.
15

How I explain things

From the café floor, to the dollar.

  • Analogy from the trade. I explain a new idea with something already on the bench. A dose. An invoice. A quiet hour.
  • One number carries it. $20 a day becomes $7,300 a year. The right single figure explains more than a paragraph.
  • Plain words for expert ideas. Yield, drift, resolution: I use the real concept but never the jargon word if a plain one exists.
  • Show the working. I don't ask you to trust the number. I show how it was built, so you can check it yourself.
  • Stop when it's clear. The moment it lands, I stop. Over-explaining is a way of not trusting the reader.

The phrases I own

Use these like signatures. They're mine, they're consistent, and they carry the whole worldview.

Busy doesn't mean profitable. The drift is the leak. Takings are loud. Margin is quiet. Compare it to your own history, not your memory. We refuse to guess. Send me three months of files. I'll tell you what they say. Twice the fee, or you don't pay. Every Monday, know exactly where your profit leaked.
16

Make it exactly me

Five things only you can answer.

Everything above is drawn from how you already show up. These are the gaps I can't fill honestly. Answer any of them and the Founder Brain stops being a strong draft and becomes unmistakably yours.

  • The real turning-point moment. The specific week, number or café where you first saw the leak. The true version replaces the narrative one.
  • A number you're willing to name. If you'll say what a mistake actually cost you, your story stops being a bio and becomes a hook people screenshot.
  • Words you love and words you hate. Add to the "words I don't use" list, and tell me the ones that are unmistakably you.
  • How blunt you want to be. Where's your line between honest and too honest? I'll calibrate the edge to yours.
  • What you never want to be seen as. The one perception you'd hate. I'll write to avoid it every time.
